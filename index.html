The question

Why this matters, and who it affects.

Alzheimer's affects over 50 million people worldwide and is the most common cause of dementia. By the time most patients are diagnosed, the disease has already progressed: the memory slips, the missed appointments, the personality changes are noticed long before the clinical diagnosis arrives, and the window for early intervention has narrowed.

So the question was deliberately practical: can routine clinical data, collected at any standard visit, reveal patterns that help detect Alzheimer's earlier? The goal was never raw prediction. It was interpretability: a model a clinician can read, audit, and trust.

Chapter one · exploration

Functional decline matters more than cognitive tests alone.

Who is in the data

A realistic class balance

35.4% of patients carry an Alzheimer's diagnosis, a 1.83:1 imbalance that mirrors clinical reality. That shaped everything downstream: stratified sampling and class-weighted models throughout.

Figure 1 · diagnosis distribution

What predicts a diagnosis

Daily-living ability outranks the memory test

The strongest signals are FunctionalAssessment (r = −0.36) and ADL (r = −0.33), measures of how well a person manages daily life, which outrank even MMSE (r = −0.24). Subjective MemoryComplaints ranks third (r = +0.31), validating the clinical wisdom of listening to patients and families. No single feature exceeds r = 0.36: no one test is enough.

Chapter two · clustering

When the model "failed," it told us something true.

Do discrete subtypes exist?

No. And that is the finding.

I tested k-means for k = 2 through 7 and validated with four metrics plus hierarchical clustering. Every signal agreed: silhouette scores of 0.05 to 0.06 (far below the 0.30 threshold), no elbow, and k-means versus hierarchical agreeing almost not at all (ARI = 0.025). The clusters were algorithmic artifacts, not real structure.

Rather than force it, I reframed it: Alzheimer's severity here lies on a continuum, not in discrete subtypes, consistent with the NIA-AA framework. No method will find what does not exist, and recognizing that takes rigor.

Figure 3 · silhouette across k · no real separation

Chapter three · association rules

Which combinations of symptoms predict a diagnosis?

Since no subtypes existed, the question shifted: not "what type of patient is this?" but "when these symptoms co-occur, how predictive is that?" The Apriori algorithm surfaced 39 interpretable rules, each at 60% confidence or higher with lift ≥ 1.5. The strongest:

IF MemoryComplaints AND MMSE = severe impairment → Alzheimer's diagnosis

The top three antecedents are all behavioral or subjective, not blood tests or imaging. Patient and caregiver-reported memory concerns are not "soft" data; they are among the strongest signals available.

What the rules are built from

Three signals carry the rules

Across all 39 rules, MemoryComplaints appears far more than anything else, followed by severe MMSE and BehavioralProblems. The two highlighted here also dominate the decision tree, the first hint of convergence.

Chapter four · the model

A model a clinician can actually read.

A grid search across 12 configurations landed on a decision tree (max depth 5, minimum 10 samples per leaf): 93.8% accuracy, 0.912 F1, 91% sensitivity, 95% specificity, fully transparent, with a reasoning path you can follow for any patient.

Convergence

Three independent methods, the same answer.

The strongest validation is not one number; it is agreement. MemoryComplaints and BehavioralProblems surface as important in correlation analysis, in the association rules, and in the decision-tree splits, three methods that share no assumptions, pointing to the same clinically central signals.

The findings

Six things this revealed.

93.8%

Interpretable models work

A transparent tree beats logistic regression by 12 points. Black-box models are not required for excellent clinical prediction.

Continuum

No discrete subtypes

Silhouette ≈ 0.06, ARI = 0.025: Alzheimer's severity varies continuously, consistent with current biological frameworks.

84%

One actionable rule

Memory complaints plus severe MMSE impairment gives 84% confidence of diagnosis: a usable screening guideline.

FunctionalAssessment (23.3%) beats MMSE (21.2%) across every method. Daily-living ability may be the earlier indicator.
